Cyber Threat Library

Learn about the most common cyber threats and how to protect yourself.

Phishing ▼

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of online scam where cybercriminals pretend to be trusted people or companies (like your bank, a delivery service, or even your boss) to trick you into sharing private information. This information can include things like your usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, or even personal details such as your date of birth or address.

These attackers usually reach out to you through emails, text messages, phone calls, or even fake websites that look very real. Their main goal is to steal your information so they can use it for things like stealing your money, hacking into your accounts, or committing identity theft.

How to Recognize a Phishing Attempt:

  • Strange email addresses: The sender’s address may look similar to a real one, but it usually has extra numbers, letters, or spelling mistakes (for example: support@paypa1.com instead of paypal.com).
  • Scary or urgent messages: The message may say things like “Your account will be locked today!” or “Act now to avoid losing access.” This is to make you panic and click without thinking.
  • Requests for personal details: No real company will ask you to send your password, PIN, or credit card number through email or text.
  • Suspicious links: The email may ask you to click a link that looks official but actually takes you to a fake website. When you hover your mouse over the link (without clicking), you may notice the web address looks unusual or misspelled.
  • Attachments you weren’t expecting: A random file may contain malware (harmful software) if you download and open it.

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Think before you click: Never click on links or download files from suspicious emails or messages.
  • Double-check the sender: Look carefully at the email address or phone number. If something feels off, contact the company directly using their official website or phone number.
  • Use Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): This adds an extra step when logging into your accounts (like entering a code sent to your phone). Even if a hacker gets your password, they still can’t get in easily.
  • Keep your software updated: Make sure your phone, computer, and apps always have the latest security updates installed.
  • Report phishing attempts: If you receive a suspicious message, report it to the company being impersonated and delete it. Most email services also let you mark the message as "Phishing".

Remember: if a message seems unusual, too good to be true, or tries to scare you into acting fast, i'’s probably a phishing attempt.

Ransomware ▼

What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of harmful software (malware) that locks you out of your computer or files. Once your files are locked, you can’t open or use them without a special "key." The criminals behind the attack then demand that you pay them money (called a ransom) to get the key and unlock your files.

These payments are usually asked for in cryptocurrencies (like Bitcoin) so they can’t be traced easily. Even if you pay, there’s no guarantee the attackers will actually unlock your files — many victims pay and never get their data back.

How to Recognize a Ransomware Attack:

  • Sudden inability to access files: Files that were working fine before may suddenly show errors, be renamed with strange extensions (like .locked), or become completely unreadable.
  • A ransom note appears: A pop-up message or a text/HTML file may appear on your screen telling you your files are locked. It usually demands payment and gives instructions on how to pay.
  • Threats to scare you: The message may say your files will be deleted, published online, or made permanentely inaccessible if you don’t pay within a short time limit.
  • Computer running strangely: Your system may slow down, crash, or show unusual error messages after the infection.

How to Protect Yourself from Ransomware:

  • Back up your files regularly: Keep copies of your important data on an external drive or cloud service. If your computer is infected, you can restore your files from the backup.
  • Update your software: Make sure your operating system, apps, and antivirus software are always up to date. Updates often fix security holes that attackers try to exploit.
  • Avoid suspicious downloads: Don’t download files from unknown websites or click links in random emails. These are common ways ransomware spreads.
  • Be careful with email attachments: Even if the email looks like it’s from someone you know, double-check before opening attachments — especially if you weren’t expecting them.
  • Use trusted security software: A good antivirus or anti-malware program can detect and block ransomware before it can do damage.
  • Think twice before enabling macros: Some ransomware hides in Word or Excel documents that ask you to "enable macros." Never do this unless you’re 100% sure the file is safe.

Remember: once ransomware locks your files, recovering them is very hard. Prevention is the best protection — stay alert and back up your data often.

Malware ▼

What is Malware?

Malware (short for malicious software) is any type of harmful program created to damage, steal from, or take control of your computer, phone, or network. Unlike normal software that helps you, malware is made to harm you or your device.

There are many different types of malware, such as:

  • Viruses: Programs that spread by attaching themselves to files and can damage or delete data.
  • Worms: Malware that spreads on its own through networks without needing you to click anything.
  • Trojans: Programs that pretend to be safe or useful but secretly cause harm once installed.
  • Spyware: Software that secretly monitors your activity and sends your personal data to hackers.
  • Adware: Unwanted programs that flood your device with pop-up ads, often slowing it down.
  • Ransomware: A type of malware that locks your files and demands payment to unlock them.

How to Recognize a Malware Infection:

  • Slower performance: Your computer or phone suddenly becomes much slower than usual.
  • Unexpected pop-ups or ads: You see lots of strange messages or ads, even when you aren’t browsing the internet.
  • Programs behaving oddly: Apps might open, close, or crash without you doing anything.
  • Unauthorized access: Files may disappear, move around, or be changed without your knowledge.
  • Strange network activity: Your internet might feel unusually busy even when you aren’t using it, as malware may be sending information in the background.
  • Battery draining fast (for phones): Malware running secretly can use up your phone’s battery much faster than normal.

How to Protect Yourself from Malware:

  • Use trusted antivirus software: A good security program can detect and remove malware before it causes serious damage.
  • Keep your system updated: Regular updates fix weaknesses in your operating system and apps that hackers might exploit.
  • Avoid suspicious links and downloads: Don’t click unknown links or download files from emails, texts, or websites you don’t trust.
  • Be careful with free software: Some “free” apps can come bundled with hidden malware. Only download from official app stores or trusted sites.
  • Check before plugging in USB drives: Hackers can use infected USB drives to spread malware to your computer.
  • Use a firewall: Firewalls help block unauthorized access to your system over the internet.

Remember: Malware can sneak onto your device without you even noticing. Staying cautious and keeping your software updated is the best defense.

Social Engineering ▼

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is a trick used by cybercriminals to get you to share private or sensitive information. Instead of breaking into your computer with software, they try to manipulate you as a person into giving them access. They often pretend to be someone you trust, such as a bank worker, a company employee, or even a friend, to get you to reveal details like your passwords, banking information, or personal data.

Social engineering attacks can happen through emails, phone calls, text messages, or even in person. The attackers rely on human emotions like fear, trust, urgency, or curiosity to trick you.

Common Examples of Social Engineering:

  • Phishing emails: Fake emails that look like they’re from a real company asking you to “confirm” your account details.
  • Pretexting: When someone creates a fake story (or “pretext”) to get you to share information — for example, pretending to be IT support asking for your login to “fix an issue.”
  • Baiting: Offering something tempting, like a free download or USB drive, that actually contains malware.
  • Tailgating (or Piggybacking): Physically following someone into a secure building by pretending to be an employee who forgot their badge.
  • Vishing (Voice Phishing): Phone calls from scammers pretending to be from your bank or a government agency, trying to make you give personal information.

How to Recognize a Social Engineering Attack:

  • Unusual requests: Someone asks for sensitive information (like passwords or account details) that they normally wouldn’t need.
  • Pressure to act quickly: The attacker tries to rush you, saying things like “Your account will be closed today if you don’t act now.”
  • Impersonation of authority figures: They may pretend to be a boss, police officer, or bank representative to gain your trust.
  • Too good to be true offers: Promises of free money, prizes, or gifts are usually scams.
  • Requests to keep it secret: If someone tells you “Don’t tell anyone about this,” it’s a red flag.

How to Protect Yourself from Social Engineering:

  • Be skeptical of unsolicited requests: If someone contacts you out of the blue asking for private details, don’t trust it right away.
  • Verify identities: Call the company or person directly using a phone number or website you know is real — not the one given in the suspicious message.
  • Never share sensitive info without confirmation: Don’t give out passwords, PINs, or financial details unless you are 100% sure the request is genuine.
  • Take your time: Don’t let anyone rush you. Scammers want you to act before thinking carefully.
  • Educate yourself and others: The more you know about social engineering tactics, the less likely you are to fall for them.

Remember: Social engineering attacks target people, not just computers. Stay alert, trust your instincts, and always double-check before sharing personal information.

Wi-Fi Risks ▼

What are Wi-Fi Risks?

Using public or unsecured Wi-Fi networks (like the free Wi-Fi in airports, cafes, hotels, or shopping malls) can put your personal information at risk. These networks are often open for anyone to join, which means attackers can secretly watch what you’re doing online.

Cybercriminals can use these networks to steal your passwords, read your messages, spy on the websites you visit, or even take control of your accounts. Because public Wi-Fi doesn’t always use strong encryption, your data may travel across the network in a way that ’s easy to intercept.

How Attackers Exploit Public Wi-Fi:

  • Man-in-the-Middle Attacks: Hackers place themselves between you and the internet, secretly copying the information you send and receive.
  • Fake Wi-Fi Hotspots: Attackers create a Wi-Fi network with a name that looks legitimate (like “Free_Airport_WiFi”) to trick you into connecting.
  • Data Sniffing: Without proper encryption, hackers can monitor your online activity and steal login details, credit card numbers, or private messages.
  • Session Hijacking: Once you log in to a website, attackers can hijack your session and access your account as if they were you.

How to Recognize Risky Wi-Fi Networks:

  • No password required: Networks that don’t ask for a password are usually unsafe.
  • Unfamiliar or suspicious names: Be cautious if you see networks with strange or misspelled names (e.g., “Sturbucks_FreeWiFi” instead of “Starbucks WiFi”).
  • Security warning messages: If your device warns that the connection isn’t secure, don’t ignore it.
  • Too good to be true: A network that suddenly offers fast, free Wi-Fi everywhere may not be trustworthy.

How to Protect Yourself from Wi-Fi Risks:

  • Avoid doing sensitive tasks on public Wi-Fi: Don’t log in to your bank, shop online, or access private accounts when using open Wi-Fi.
  • Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network): A VPN encrypts your internet connection so hackers can’t easily see what you’re doing.
  • Connect only to trusted networks: Whenever possible, use mobile data or secure Wi-Fi you know belongs to the place you’re in.
  • Forget networks after use: Set your device so it doesn’t automatically reconnect to Wi-Fi networks without your permission.
  • Keep your device updated: Updates often include security fixes that protect you from Wi-Fi attacks.
  • Turn off sharing: Disable features like file sharing, printer sharing, and AirDrop when on public Wi-Fi.

Remember: Free Wi-Fi is convenient, but it comes with risks. Always think twice before connecting, and use extra protection like a VPN if you need to use it.

Data Privacy & GDPR ▼

What is Data Privacy & GDPR?

Data Privacy is about protecting your personal information — things like your name, email address, phone number, home address, photos, and even your online activity. Protecting your privacy means making sure your information is not collected, shared, or used without your knowledge or permission.

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) is a law in the European Union (EU) designed to give people more control over their personal data. Even if you don’t live in the EU, many companies around the world follow GDPR rules, so it can still benefit you. GDPR makes it clear that your data belongs to you, and you have the right to know how it’s collected, stored, and used.

Why Data Privacy Matters:

  • Prevents identity theft: If your private info is stolen, criminals could open bank accounts, credit cards, or loans in your name.
  • Protects your money: Hackers can use stolen data to access your bank accounts or shop online without your permission.
  • Keeps your online life private: Companies and hackers shouldn’t be able to track your every move without your consent.
  • Avoids unwanted spam and scams: Sharing too much data can lead to endless spam emails, calls, and targeted scams.

Your Rights Under GDPR:

  • Right to know: You can ask companies what data they have about you.
  • Right to consent: Companies must ask for your permission before collecting or using your data.
  • Right to access: You can request a copy of the personal data a company has stored about you.
  • Right to delete: You can ask for your data to be erased (“right to be forgotten”).
  • Right to correct: If your information is wrong, you can have it fixed.
  • Right to limit or refuse: You can say no to certain ways companies use your data, like for marketing.

How to Recognize GDPR in Action:

  • Consent requests: Websites asking you to accept or reject cookies before browsing.
  • Privacy policy updates: Emails or pop-ups from services explaining how your data is handled.
  • Options to manage your data: Settings in your account that let you download, delete, or change your personal information.

How to Protect Your Data Privacy:

  • Read privacy policies: Before using a website or app, check how your data will be collected and used.
  • Use privacy settings: Adjust the privacy options on social media, apps, and devices to control who can see your information.
  • Be careful with what you share: Don’t post sensitive details like your address, phone number, or travel plans on public platforms.
  • Exercise your rights: If a company misuses your data, request corrections or deletion using your GDPR rights.
  • Use strong security practices: Protect your accounts with strong passwords, two-factor authentication, and up-to-date software.

Remember: Your personal data is valuable. Treat it like money — only share it with trusted companies and always make sure you’re in control of how it’s used.
